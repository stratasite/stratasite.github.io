## Speed Is All You Need

Attention got us the models. Speed is what decides whether they're usable on your data. Any conversation with an agent 
can fan out into dozens of warehouse queries. Even a single prompt is often 3 or more queries, some just probing for 
context. If your warehouse answers in 15 seconds on average, that's 45 seconds of query time for one prompt. Add reasoning 
and network latency and you're looking at minutes per prompt.

That's fatal for agents on data, and unacceptable for interactive dashboards.

The natural reaction is to "cube" your data into isolated OLAP tiers. But a cube only answers the questions you anticipated 
when you built it, and the question space an LLM explores is open-ended. So stakeholders are constantly waiting on new cubes 
or fixes to old ones. Data engineers end up maintaining thousands of them with divergent measure definitions, and neither 
humans nor agents can self-serve the right measures without going through engineering.

Strata solves this with a semantic layer that spans compute engines. You tactically place hot, critical data in high-performance 
OLAP engines while keeping deep access to rarely-touched data in cold storage, all under one measure model. 
The result: far fewer redundant aggregates, self-service over rare data without burdening engineering, and sub-second latency on 
the dashboards and queries that matter.

## Aggregate Aware across Compute Engines

Most tools that are "aggregate-aware" stop at one question: is there a pre-aggregated table that can satisfy this query? 
Strata asks a second one. Of the tables that can, which lives on the fastest engine? Because Strata federates across
multiple compute engines under a single measure model, it can hold the same aggregate in more than one place. 

You might keep a hot-tier copy in ClickHouse and a cold-tier copy in another engine. When a query could be answered by 
either, Strata preferentially routes to the faster source, then falls back automatically when filters 
fall outside the hot tier's range. You get the speed of a purpose-built OLAP engine with the coverage of your warehouse, 
and no one has to hand-pick the source.

## Open OLAP 

Strata brings a fundamentally open approach to OLAP for business intelligence. Traditionally, OLAP lives in proprietary, 
closed tiers: you build a cube, and it's reachable only through that vendor's API. Open OLAP inverts this. You bring any 
compute engine suited to the workload, whether that's a time-series engine like Druid or a generalist like ClickHouse, and 
Strata models across all of them under one measure definition.

The payoff is that your high-performance investments stay yours. The aggregate tables Strata builds live in open, SQL-native 
engines, not behind a proprietary cube API, so the same hot-tier infrastructure can serve other workloads instead of sitting 
locked to one tool. That frees you from vendor lock-in, cuts duplicate data and pipelines, and maximizes the return on the
OLAP infrastructure you've already paid for.

## Partition-Aware Routing

A hot tier is only useful if you can trust it to answer the right questions. Push a query to ClickHouse for a date range it 
doesn't hold, and you get a fast answer that's wrong, the worst outcome of all.

Strata's partition logic makes the semantic model aware of the data range each source holds. It routes to the hot tier only 
when the user's filters fall inside that range, and falls back to the warehouse of record otherwise. You get sub-second 
dashboards and agent turns when the hot tier can serve the query, correct results when it can't, and reduced load and compute
cost on the warehouse either way.

<Callout type="tip" title="Sizing the savings">
  Cost reduction is workload-dependent, strongest when a meaningful share of traffic is repetitive queries over recent or aggregated data a hot tier can absorb. Size it against your own query history.
</Callout>
